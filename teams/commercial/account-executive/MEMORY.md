## MEDDIC Discovery Framework

### Metrics (M)
What does success look like in numbers for this prospect?

**Examples:**
- Dental: "Reduce manual appointment scheduling time by 50% (from 10h/week to 5h/week)"
- Health: "Eliminate 200 manual data entry hours/month across 3 departments"
- Finance: "Target ROI: 3:1 within 12 months"

**Your job:** Find the specific number that matters to them.

### Economic Buyer (E)
Who signs the contract and holds the budget?

**Dental:** Usually the practice owner or practice manager (tight decision-making)
**Health:** Usually department head or IT director (formal approval chain)
**Key:** Confirm they have unilateral authority; if not, identify who they need to convince

### Decision Criteria (D)
What are their formal evaluation criteria?

**Examples:**
- GDPR compliance certified
- Can integrate with our existing system
- Proven track record in dental/health
- Support availability (email / phone / on-site)
- Pricing <EUR 400/month

**Your job:** Ask them explicitly. They often have a formal RFP or checklist.

### Decision Process (D)
Who is involved, what is the approval process, what is the timeline?

**Dental:** Often a 2–4 week trial + decision, informal approval
**Health:** Formal committee, budget approval, compliance review (6–8 weeks)
**Key:** Map the exact people and timeline.

### Identify Pain (I)
What specifically breaks or costs them if they don't solve this?

**Financial pain:** Cost of manual hours, error corrections, compliance fines
**Operational pain:** Staff time, system errors, customer complaints
**Compliance pain:** GDPR exposure, audit risk, liability

**Your job:** Get specific. "Better workflows" is not pain. "Manual appointment entry takes 3h daily and causes 10% no-show rate" is pain.

### Champion (C)
Who internally wants this to happen and will advocate for you?

**Dental:** Sometimes the office manager pushing for better tools
**Health:** Usually a department head or IT champion frustrated with status quo

**Key:** Identify them early. They become your ally when negotiation gets tough.

## Sales Cycle Dynamics

### Dental Practices (Faster, 30–60 days)
- Decision-maker is usually accessible (owner or manager)
- Budget is often discretionary (2–5% of revenue)
- GDPR angle resonates (they know they're exposed)
- Pain point is clear (manual scheduling, patient data chaos)
- Buying process is informal (no committee, no RFP)
- Objection: "We already have [existing system]"
  - **Response:** "Delphi isn't replacing your system. It's the GDPR compliance + automation layer on top. Your existing system stays; Delphi makes it safer and faster."

### Health Clinics/Hospitals (Slower, 60–90 days)
- Decision-maker is harder to access (formal structure)
- Budget is formal, allocated annually (committee approves)
- GDPR angle is critical (they're under strict compliance)
- Pain point requires discovery (may not be obvious at first)
- Buying process is formal (RFP, committee, compliance review)
- Objection: "We are already compliant"
  - **Response:** "Are you audited regularly? Can you show us your GDPR audit report? Most health organizations are compliant on paper but exposed in process. Delphi closes that gap."

## Discovery Patterns That Worked

### Strong Discovery Signals
- Prospect has experienced a data breach or compliance concern recently
- Prospect has recently hired a compliance officer or operations lead
- Prospect is losing staff to manual, repetitive work
- Prospect is growing and scaling; current system is straining
- Prospect mentions they've been looking at similar tools (they have budget)

### Weak Discovery Signals
- Prospect is "exploring options" with no urgency (no timeline)
- Prospect has just implemented a new system (locked in for 2+ years)
- Prospect says "we're happy with our current solution" AND no GDPR concern
- Prospect is in a hiring freeze (likely budget freeze too)

## Objection Response Patterns

### "We need to think about it"
**Don't accept at face value.** Dig:
- "What specifically would help you decide? Is it the investment, the timeline, the technical fit?"
- Identify the real blocker. Is it budget? Authority? Internal approval? Understanding?
- Address the blocker directly, not the surface objection.

### Price Objection ("Too expensive")
1. **Reframe value first.** "This prospect pays 50h/month in manual scheduling. At €25/h, that's €1,250/month. Our service saves them 25h/week. Do the math."
2. **Never lead with discount.** Always anchor to ROI.
3. **If they insist:** Flag to HERMES + Finance. "Prospect is price-sensitive; margin impact if we discount."

### Competitor Mentioned
1. **Never attack the competitor.** Ask, "What's drawing you to them?"
2. **Listen.** Their answer tells you what matters most to them.
3. **Reposition on Delphi's unique angle.** "We're the GDPR layer; they're the general automation tool. For dental/health, compliance is non-negotiable."

### Ghost After Proposal
- **48h:** "Did this reach you? Any questions on our end?"
- **5 days:** Follow up based on what they read (if visible). Reframe based on their focus area.
- **10 days:** "Any blockers or concerns I can help with?"
- **>10 days:** Flag to HERMES. Don't send another message without approval.

## Email & Messaging Patterns

### Great First Email (After Warm Handoff from SDR)
- Opens with a specific reference to their pain (not generic)
- One clear ask (e.g., "15-min call Thursday?")
- Proof point relevant to their sector
- No sales language; sounds like a person

### Great Follow-Up (After Proposal)
- References what they read in the proposal (if visible)
- Addresses their specific concern (reframe, don't apologize)
- One clear ask (e.g., "Still on for a decision by end of week?")
